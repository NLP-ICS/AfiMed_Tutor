**Part 2 — Why the correct answer is correct:**

This clinical scenario is a classic presentation of **severe Aortic Stenosis (AS)**, and recognizing the triad of symptoms is key:

- **Syncope** (sudden loss of consciousness)
- **Angina** (substernal chest pressure on exertion)
- **Heart failure** (shortness of breath, bibasilar rales, suggesting pulmonary congestion)

These three symptoms together — often remembered as **SAD: Syncope, Angina, Dyspnea** — are the hallmark warning signs of critical aortic stenosis in an elderly patient.

The pathophysiology is straightforward: the aortic valve becomes calcified and narrowed over decades (especially in patients over 70), creating significant obstruction to left ventricular outflow. Blood is forced through a tight orifice, generating turbulent flow that produces the characteristic murmur.

The classic auscultatory finding of aortic stenosis is:
> **A harsh, systolic crescendo-decrescendo (ejection) murmur, heard best at the upper right sternal border (aortic area, 2nd intercostal space), often radiating to the carotid arteries.**

The murmur is crescendo-decrescendo because it mirrors the pressure gradient across the valve — building as ventricular pressure rises and fading as it falls during systole.

Additional bedside clues supporting severe AS include:
- A **slow-rising, low-amplitude pulse** (pulsus parvus et tardus)
- A **sustained, heaving apex beat** (due to left ventricular hypertrophy)
- Possible **loss of A2** (aortic component of the second heart sound) in severe disease
